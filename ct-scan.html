Introduction

Computed Tomography (CT) scan is a medical imaging technique that uses X-rays and computer processing to generate cross-sectional images of the human body. It provides more detailed information than conventional X-ray imaging.

Basic Principle of CT Scan

CT scanning is based on the differential absorption of X-rays by various tissues in the body. An X-ray tube rotates around the patient, and detectors measure the attenuated X-rays from multiple angles to reconstruct images.

Main Components of CT Scanner

  • X-ray Tube: Generates X-ray beam
  • Detectors: Measure transmitted X-rays
  • Gantry: Houses rotating components
  • Patient Table: Moves patient through scanner
  • Computer System: Image reconstruction

Image Reconstruction

CT images are reconstructed using mathematical algorithms such as Filtered Back Projection (FBP) or Iterative Reconstruction. These techniques convert raw projection data into detailed cross-sectional images.

Advantages of CT Scan

  • Fast imaging speed
  • High spatial resolution
  • Excellent visualization of bones and organs
  • Useful in emergency diagnosis

Limitations of CT Scan

  • Uses ionizing radiation
  • Higher radiation dose compared to X-ray
  • Not suitable for frequent imaging

Applications of CT Scan

  • Trauma and emergency diagnosis
  • Brain and chest imaging
  • Cancer detection and staging
  • Angiography
  • Guided biopsy procedures
