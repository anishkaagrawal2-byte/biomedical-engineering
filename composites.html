Introduction

Composite biomaterials are formed by combining two or more different materials to obtain superior mechanical, biological, and chemical properties. In biomedical engineering, composites are designed to mimic natural tissues such as bone.

Why Composites Are Used in Biomedical Engineering

  • Single materials cannot meet all biological and mechanical requirements
  • Improved strength-to-weight ratio
  • Tailored bioactivity and degradation behavior
  • Better similarity to natural tissues

Classification of Biocomposites

1. Polymer Matrix Composites

These composites use polymers as the matrix material reinforced with ceramic or fiber components.

  • PLA + Hydroxyapatite
  • Chitosan + Bioactive glass

2. Ceramic Matrix Composites

Ceramic matrix composites are reinforced with fibers or particles to improve fracture toughness.

  • Alumina reinforced zirconia
  • Calcium phosphate composites

3. Metal Matrix Composites

These composites combine metals with ceramics or polymers to enhance wear resistance and bioactivity.

  • Titanium + Hydroxyapatite
  • Magnesium-based composites

Common Biocomposite Systems

Hydroxyapatite–Polymer Composites

These composites are widely used in bone tissue engineering due to their excellent osteoconductivity and flexibility.

  • Applications: Bone scaffolds, coatings
  • Advantages: Bone-like behavior
  • Limitations: Complex fabrication

Carbon Fiber Reinforced Composites

Carbon fiber composites provide high strength and radiolucency, making them suitable for orthopedic implants.

  • Applications: Prosthetics, fixation devices
  • Advantages: High strength, lightweight
  • Limitations: High cost

Applications of Biocomposites

  • Bone tissue engineering
  • Orthopedic and dental implants
  • Prosthetics and fixation devices
  • Drug delivery systems

Advantages of Biocomposites

  • Customizable properties
  • Improved mechanical performance
  • Better tissue compatibility

Limitations and Challenges

  • Complex manufacturing processes
  • Higher cost
  • Interfacial bonding issues
