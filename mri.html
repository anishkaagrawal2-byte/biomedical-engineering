Introduction

Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) is a non-invasive medical imaging technique that uses strong magnetic fields and radiofrequency pulses to generate detailed images of internal body structures. Unlike X-rays and CT scans, MRI does not use ionizing radiation.

Basic Principle of MRI

MRI is based on the principle of Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR). When the human body is placed inside a strong magnetic field, hydrogen nuclei align with the field. Radiofrequency pulses disturb this alignment, and the emitted signals during relaxation are used to form images.

Physics Behind MRI (NMR)

  • Alignment of hydrogen protons in magnetic field
  • RF excitation at Larmor frequency
  • T1 (spin-lattice) relaxation
  • T2 (spin-spin) relaxation
  • Signal detection by receiver coils

Main Components of MRI System

  • Main Magnet: Produces strong static magnetic field
  • Gradient Coils: Spatial encoding of signals
  • RF Coils: Transmit and receive RF signals
  • Computer System: Image reconstruction
  • Cryogenic System: Cooling superconducting magnets

Image Formation in MRI

Image formation involves spatial encoding using gradient magnetic fields and signal processing through Fourier Transform to produce high-resolution images.

Advantages of MRI

  • Excellent soft tissue contrast
  • No ionizing radiation
  • Multiplanar imaging capability
  • High spatial resolution

Limitations of MRI

  • High cost
  • Long scan times
  • Not suitable for patients with metal implants
  • Claustrophobia issues

Applications of MRI

  • Brain and spinal cord imaging
  • Musculoskeletal imaging
  • Cardiac MRI
  • Oncology diagnostics
  • Functional MRI (fMRI)
