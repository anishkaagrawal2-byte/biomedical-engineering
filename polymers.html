Introduction

Polymeric biomaterials are widely used in biomedical engineering due to their flexibility, lightweight nature, and ability to be tailored for specific medical applications. They can be designed to be biodegradable or non-biodegradable depending on clinical requirements.

Important Properties of Biomedical Polymers

  • Low density and flexibility
  • Biocompatibility
  • Controlled degradation rate
  • Ease of processing and fabrication
  • Chemical stability

Classification of Polymeric Biomaterials

1. Natural Polymers

Natural polymers are derived from biological sources and generally exhibit excellent biocompatibility.

  • Collagen
  • Gelatin
  • Chitosan
  • Alginate

2. Synthetic Polymers

Synthetic polymers offer better control over mechanical and chemical properties compared to natural polymers.

  • Polyethylene (PE)
  • Polylactic Acid (PLA)
  • Polyglycolic Acid (PGA)
  • Polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE)
  • Silicone Rubber

Common Polymers Used in Biomedical Engineering

Polylactic Acid (PLA)

PLA is a biodegradable polymer widely used in tissue engineering and drug delivery systems.

  • Applications: Sutures, drug delivery, scaffolds
  • Advantages: Biodegradable, biocompatible
  • Limitations: Brittle nature

Polyethylene Glycol (PEG)

PEG is a hydrophilic polymer used to improve biocompatibility and circulation time of drugs.

  • Applications: Drug delivery, surface modification
  • Advantages: Non-toxic, water soluble
  • Limitations: Low mechanical strength

Polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE)

PTFE is a non-degradable polymer known for its chemical inertness and low friction.

  • Applications: Vascular grafts, catheters
  • Advantages: Excellent chemical resistance
  • Limitations: Non-biodegradable

Silicone Rubber

Silicone rubber is widely used due to its flexibility and long-term stability inside the human body.

  • Applications: Implants, tubing, prosthetics
  • Advantages: Flexible, durable
  • Limitations: Low mechanical strength

Advantages of Polymeric Biomaterials

  • Highly versatile material properties
  • Ease of shaping and processing
  • Suitable for temporary implants

Limitations and Challenges

  • Lower strength compared to metals
  • Possible degradation by-products
  • Aging and wear over time
