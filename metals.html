Introduction

Metallic biomaterials are widely used in biomedical engineering due to their high mechanical strength, durability, and ability to withstand physiological loads. They are commonly used in orthopedic, dental, and cardiovascular applications.

Important Properties of Biomedical Metals

  • High mechanical strength
  • Excellent fatigue resistance
  • Corrosion resistance in body fluids
  • Biocompatibility
  • Good wear resistance

Common Metals Used in Biomedical Engineering

1. Stainless Steel (316L)

Stainless steel is commonly used for temporary implants and surgical instruments due to its good strength and corrosion resistance.

  • Applications: Bone plates, screws, surgical tools
  • Advantages: Low cost, easy fabrication
  • Limitations: Possible corrosion over long-term use

2. Titanium and Titanium Alloys

Titanium exhibits excellent biocompatibility and corrosion resistance, making it ideal for permanent implants.

  • Applications: Hip implants, dental implants, pacemakers
  • Advantages: Lightweight, osseointegration
  • Limitations: High cost

3. Cobalt-Chromium Alloys

These alloys are known for their superior wear resistance and strength, especially in load-bearing implants.

  • Applications: Knee and hip joint replacements
  • Advantages: Excellent wear resistance
  • Limitations: Higher stiffness may cause stress shielding

4. Magnesium Alloys

Magnesium alloys are biodegradable metals that gradually dissolve in the body, making them suitable for temporary implants.

  • Applications: Temporary bone fixation devices
  • Advantages: Biodegradable, similar density to bone
  • Limitations: Rapid corrosion rate

Advantages of Metallic Biomaterials

  • High load-bearing capability
  • Long service life
  • Suitable for complex mechanical applications

Limitations and Challenges

  • Corrosion and ion release
  • Stress shielding effect
  • Potential allergic reactions
